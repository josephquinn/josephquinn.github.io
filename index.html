My primary research interests consider beliefs about status, how these beliefs change, and when they facilitate or constrain opportunities for particular groups. I also study intergroup dynamics and segregation in a variety of settings, from schools and neighborhoods to digital venues.

Current projects of mine explore teacher-student interactions in secondary schools after adolescents change schools in a residential mobility program, the role of status characteristics in shaping the opportunities available to creatives and professionals, and ways that organizational structure can facilitate meaningful intergroup contact. A portion of my research agenda involves methodological projects on both the survey items and computational approaches used to measure and model networks.

Before arriving at Duke I taught physics at McKinley Technology High School in DC (go Tech!), and worked as a technical researcher at MDRC in support of several large-scale program evaluations funded by the Institute of Educational Sciences, the US Department of Education, and the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation. I also supported projects for the Poverty and Inequality Research Lab in Baltimore, and wrote programs for social science lab groups at Johns Hopkins and Yale.
