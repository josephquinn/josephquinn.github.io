My primary research interests consider beliefs about status, how these beliefs change, and when they facilitate or constrain opportunities for particular groups. I also study intergroup dynamics and segregation in a variety of settings, from schools and neighborhoods to digital and public spaces.

Current projects of mine explore teacher-student interactions in secondary schools after adolescents experience significant neighborhood change, the role of status characteristics in shaping opportunity structures available to cultural producers, and factors influencing forgiveness of high-status actors. A portion of my research agenda involves methodological projects on both the survey items and computational approaches used to measure and model networks.

Before arriving at Duke I taught physics at a public high school in DC, and worked as a technical researcher at MDRC in support of several national program evaluations funded by the Institute of Educational Sciences, the U.S. Department of Education, and the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation. I also supported projects for the Poverty and Inequality Research Lab in Baltimore, and wrote programs for social science lab groups at Johns Hopkins and Yale.
